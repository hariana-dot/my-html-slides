Why Physiotherapists Need to Know

🩺 Clinical Reality

  • Most MSK patients are already on analgesics when they see you
  • Understanding their meds helps you interpret pain responses
  • You can identify red flags (e.g. opioid misuse, NSAID contraindications)

🤝 Interprofessional Communication

  • Speak the same language as GPs, pharmacists, and specialists
  • Better referrals → better patient outcomes
  • Know when to recommend escalation / de-escalation

⚡ Treatment Timing

  • Schedule sessions relative to medication peak effect
  • Use "pain relief windows" for manual therapy / exercise
  • Avoid aggravating pain during medication troughs

Key point: Physios don't prescribe — but you work around and with these drugs every day.

NSAIDs Non-Steroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drugs

⚙️ Mechanism

Block COX-1 & COX-2 enzymes → reduce prostaglandin synthesis → ↓ inflammation & pain

💊 Common Examples

Ibuprofen Naproxen Diclofenac Celecoxib Meloxicam

⏱ Onset / Duration

Onset 30–60 min · Peak 1–4 hrs · Duration 4–12 hrs (varies by drug)

✅ Pros for Physio

  • Excellent for inflammatory MSK conditions
  • Reduces swelling → improves ROM access
  • Good pre-session for stiff OA patients
  • Widely available OTC (ibuprofen)

⚠️ Cautions

  • GI bleeding risk (esp. long-term)
  • Renal impairment / dehydration risk
  • CV risk (COX-2 selective)
  • Asthma exacerbation in sensitive pts
  • Not for neuropathic pain

Physio tip: Schedule hands-on work 1–2 hrs after NSAID intake for maximal anti-inflammatory effect. Warn patients about GI protection (take with food).

Paracetamol (Acetaminophen)

⚙️ Mechanism

Central COX inhibition (weak peripheral) · Also acts on cannabinoid & serotonin pathways · Not anti-inflammatory

💊 Examples

Paracetamol Panadol Tylenol

⏱ Onset / Duration

Onset 30–60 min · Peak 1–2 hrs · Duration 4–6 hrs

✅ Pros for Physio

  • Safe GI profile (good for elderly)
  • No renal / CV effects
  • Safe in pregnancy & breastfeeding
  • Good baseline analgesia
  • No drug–drug interactions with NSAIDs

⚠️ Cautions

  • Liver toxicity > 4 g/day
  • Weak analgesic — often insufficient alone
  • No anti-inflammatory effect
  • Alcohol interaction risk

Physio tip: Use as baseline pain relief for chronic pain patients. Combine with NSAIDs for additive effect without added GI risk. Monitor total daily intake.

Opioids

⚙️ Mechanism

Agonists at μ-opioid receptors in CNS & periphery → ↓ pain signal transmission, ↑ pain threshold, ↑ euphoria

💊 Examples

Codeine Tramadol Morphine Oxycodone Fentanyl

⏱ Onset / Duration

Variable: immediate-release (30 min) vs extended-release (hours)

✅ Pros for Physio

  • Powerful — effective for severe acute pain
  • Post-surgical / trauma rehab facilitation
  • Multiple formulations (oral, patch, injection)

⚠️ Major Cautions

  • Dependence & addiction risk ⚠️
  • Respiratory depression (high dose)
  • Constipation (common, dose-limiting)
  • Sedation → fall risk in elderly
  • Hyperalgesia with prolonged use
  • Opioid crisis — prescribe sparingly

Physio tip: Be aware of sedation during sessions. Use "pain relief windows" for active exercise. Never encourage abrupt cessation. Flag concerning usage patterns.

Topical Analgesics

⚙️ Mechanism

Local action at skin / superficial tissues · Minimal systemic absorption · Fewer side effects than oral

💊 Types

NSAID gel Capsaicin Lidocaine Menthol Counter-irritants

Best For

  • Localised OA (knee, hand)
  • Muscle strains / tendinopathy
  • Patients who can't take oral NSAIDs
  • Neuropathic pain (lidocaine patch, capsaicin)

Adjuvant Analgesics

Drugs primarily for other conditions, but effective for pain — especially neuropathic

Gabapentinoids

Gabapentin Pregabalin

↓ Calcium channel → ↓ neurotransmitter release

  • First-line for neuropathic pain
  • Also anxiety, sleep improvement
  • Dizziness & sedation common

Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs)

Amitriptyline Nortriptyline

↓ NE & 5-HT reuptake → modulate descending pain pathways

  • Low dose for pain (≠ depression dose)
  • Best for chronic tension / migraine
  • Anticholinergic: dry mouth, sedation

SNRIs

Duloxetine Venlafaxine

↑ 5-HT & NE → ↑ descending inhibition

  • Effective for OA & neuropathic pain
  • Also treats comorbid depression/anxiety
  • Withdrawal symptoms on cessation

Physio tip: Adjuvants take 2–6 weeks for full effect. Patients may feel worse before better. Time your sessions for when side effects (dizziness, sedation) are minimal — often morning or early afternoon.

Key Takeaways for Physiotherapists

1. Know the pain mechanism — Inflammatory pain → NSAIDs. Neuropathic → Adjuvants. Mixed → Combine. This is the single most important skill.

2. Work with the medication timeline — Schedule your hands-on and exercise interventions during the drug's peak effect window. Document response.

3. Screen for red flags — GI / renal / CV / hepatic comorbidities change the risk–benefit. Know your patient's full medication list.

4. Topicals are underused — First-line for localised OA and tendinopathy per NICE guidelines. Zero systemic side effects. Recommend them confidently.

5. Opioids: respect but don't fear — They have a role in acute severe pain and post-surgical rehab. Be aware of dependence risk, sedation, and the opioid crisis.

6. Communicate with prescribers — You see the patient more frequently than their GP. Your feedback on pain response to medication is clinically valuable.
